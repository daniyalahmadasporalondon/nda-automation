NDA Automation — User Guide

A plain-English walkthrough of the whole app, start to finish. Pick a topic on the left.

Welcome — start here

This app takes an NDA from "just arrived" all the way to "signed and filed," with an AI assistant doing the first-pass legal review for you.

An NDA (non-disclosure agreement) is the contract two companies sign to promise they'll keep each other's information confidential. Instead of handling each one by hand, this app lets you:

  • Receive NDAs that arrive by email, or upload them yourself.
  • Review them automatically — an AI reads each NDA and checks it against your company's rules (your "playbook").
  • Flag and mark up problems — it points out clauses that don't meet your standards and proposes "redlines" (suggested edits, shown like tracked changes in Word).
  • Generate brand-new NDAs from a template, filled in with the right party names, dates, and governing law.
  • Send the finished NDA out — by email, or for e-signature through DocuSign.
One word you'll see everywhere: a matter is just the app's name for one NDA and everything attached to it — the original document, the AI's review, any redlines, and its current status. When you see "matter," read "an NDA and its file."

The big picture: the life of an NDA

Every NDA takes the same journey through the app. You can follow any NDA along these six steps.

  1. It arrives (or you upload it). An NDA shows up one of three ways: it lands in your connected Gmail inbox and the app pulls it in automatically, you upload a Word or PDF file yourself, or you generate a new one from scratch in the app.
  2. The AI reviews it. The AI reads the NDA clause by clause and compares each part against your playbook — your standard rules, for example "the confidentiality period must be at most 5 years" or "the governing law must be on our approved list." This is the review.
  3. Problems get flagged and redlined. The app gives each clause a verdict — it passes, it needs review (a human should look), or it fails (a clear problem). For problem clauses, it proposes redlines: specific edits that would bring the clause in line with your rules.
  4. A human approves it. You open the NDA, look at the AI's findings, fix or accept the redlines, and mark each flagged clause Reviewed to confirm you've checked it. This unlocks the next step.
  5. It gets sent. You send the marked-up NDA back to the counterparty by email, or send a clean copy out for signature via DocuSign.
  6. It gets signed and filed. Once both sides sign, the NDA is executed. Executed agreements drop off your active board and move into the Corpus — your permanent, searchable library of signed NDAs.
The whole point: steps 2 and 3 (the AI review and redlines) used to be slow manual legal work. The app does the first pass; you stay in control at step 4.

The playbook is the single source of truth for your rules — the confidentiality period you accept, the governing laws you allow, which clauses are required, and so on. The AI doesn't invent its own standards; it judges every NDA against this playbook. When you change a rule in the Playbook tab, that's what the AI checks against going forward. Reviewing an NDA

This is the heart of the app. You upload or receive an NDA, and the app uses AI to read it, check it against your own rules, and mark up anything that needs to change.

How to issue a review

You start a review from the review workstation — the screen where one NDA is open, with the document on the left and a clause panel on the right.

  1. Open the NDA you want to review (from your inbox/board, or by uploading it).
  2. Look at the toolbar above the document. You'll see a button labelled Review.
  3. Click Review.

Here's what happens next:

  • The button changes to Reviewing… and a shimmering placeholder appears over the document while it works.
  • A message shows: "Reviewing with AI… this runs in the background and can take a couple of minutes."
  • The review runs in the background. You don't have to sit and wait — switch to another NDA, work on something else, or close the screen. The review keeps going on the server.
  • A typical review takes roughly 2 to 4 minutes (it reads the whole document clause by clause).
  • When it finishes, the workstation updates on its own with the verdicts, highlighted text, and comments. You don't need to refresh anything.
Re-running a review: the same Review button re-runs the review later (for example, after you've changed the Playbook rules). The newest result replaces the old one.

What the review actually does

In plain terms: an AI legal reviewer reads the NDA one clause at a time and checks each important clause against your Playbook. For each clause it works through a fixed method, in order:

  1. Locate — find the part of the document that covers this clause.
  2. Read carefully — read it literally, paying attention to "not," "except," "unless," and other words that flip a sentence's meaning.
  3. Apply — compare what it found against your Playbook's rule for that clause.
  4. Cite — pick out the exact sentence from the document that drives its decision.
  5. Decide — give the clause a verdict (see below).

The clauses it checks are the ones your Playbook cares about — typically Mutuality, Confidential Information, Governing Law, Term & Survival, Non-Circumvention, and Signatures. Because the list comes from the Playbook, if you add a new rule there, the review starts checking for it automatically.

A key safeguard: the AI is told to escalate to review when it's unsure, rather than guess a pass or a fail. If the wording is ambiguous, conflicting, or it can't tell, the safe answer is "a human should look at this."

The three verdicts

Every important clause gets one of three verdicts. This is the most important thing to understand on the whole screen.

PASS  "looks fine." The clause meets your Playbook's rule. Nothing to do.
Example: the governing law is England & Wales, which is on your approved list.

REVIEW  "a human should look at this before sending." The AI isn't confident enough to call it either way — the wording is ambiguous, conditional, conflicting, or depends on text it couldn't fully judge. This is not a failure.
Example: a confidentiality term worded in an unusual way that might or might not be time-limited.
What you do: read the flagged clause, decide if it's acceptable, then acknowledge it (see "the approve gate" on the Redlines tab). A Review clause blocks you from sending until a person has signed off on it.

FAIL  "a real problem, flagged and marked up." The clause clearly breaks a Playbook rule: a required clause is missing, a clause is present but wrong, or a forbidden clause is present. The app flags it and proposes a markup (a redline) to fix it.
Example: the governing law isn't one you approve — you'll see a FAIL with the exact sentence highlighted and a proposed redline replacing it with an approved law.
What you do: review the proposed fix and include it in the redline you send back (see the Redlines tab).

At the top of the panel you also see an overall verdict — a single PASS, REVIEW, or FAIL summary plus a tally like "4/6," telling you how many clauses passed. If no AI review has run yet, this reads Not reviewed with the prompt "Run Review to see verdicts" — that's normal and just means you haven't clicked Review yet.

Confidence and the double-check

You don't need to manage this, but it's good to know it's happening:

  • Along with each verdict, the AI reports how confident it is (a score behind the scenes). If its confidence on a clause is low, the app doesn't quietly trust it — it pushes that clause toward Review so a person looks.
  • The app can also run a second AI pass — a verifier — that double-checks the cases the first AI was unsure about. Think of it as a second opinion on the borderline calls.

The practical effect: borderline judgements lean toward "ask a human" rather than toward a confident-sounding but shaky pass or fail. The system is built to err on the side of flagging, not on the side of waving things through.

Why every verdict points to the text ("grounding")

The AI is required to quote the exact part of the document it's judging. This is called grounding, and it's a deliberate trust mechanism.

  • For any clause actually present in the document, the AI must cite a real, word-for-word quote from the NDA to support its verdict — whether Pass, Review, or Fail.
  • The app checks those quotes against the real document. If the AI claims a quote that doesn't actually appear, that quote is rejected.
  • If the AI can't produce a supporting quote for a present clause, its verdict is bumped up to human review and the document is blocked from sending.

Why this matters: the AI can't make things up. Every verdict you see is tied to a specific sentence you can read for yourself. Click a flagged clause and the app highlights the exact paragraph it's talking about. (The only verdicts with no quote are honest ones: a required clause that's missing, or a forbidden clause that's correctly absent — in both cases there's nothing to quote.)

When a review is slow or interrupted

Reviews take a couple of minutes, so it helps to know the difference between "still working" and "actually broken." The app draws this line carefully: a slow review is never mistaken for a failed one.

  • Still reviewing (normal): while the AI is working, the NDA shows as Reviewing… with the shimmer placeholder. A slow review is not a failed one — it just hasn't finished. The app keeps checking for the result on its own and switches to the finished results as soon as they're ready.
  • Taking a little longer than usual: if a review runs past the usual window (about 4 minutes), the message calmly switches to "Still reviewing with AI… this document is taking a little longer than usual. Hang tight — it's still running in the background." This is a reassurance, not an error — the app keeps polling.
  • Interrupted in the background ("stalled"): if a review's recorded start time gets old (the server's safety window is about 10 minutes), the app reports it as a distinct "stalled" state — "still processing / interrupted, retry" — rather than a failure. A stalled review shows a calm waiting message and a Retry button, so a review can never appear stuck forever and a slow review never fakes a red failure.
  • Failed (a real error): only a genuine, recorded error shows Review failed with a short explanation and a Retry button. Click Retry to run it again.
The takeaway: a slow review shows as still-working; only a real, recorded problem shows as failed. In every case there's a Retry button to run it again. You'll never lose your place, and a stuck review can't silently block you.

How the AI Review Works (under the hood)

You don't need any of this to use the app — the previous tab covers everything day-to-day. This tab is for the curious (and for admins): what actually happens between clicking Review and seeing the verdicts. Three stages: read the document, check each clause, and the AI layer that judges the meaning.

Stage 1 — Reading the document

Before any clause is judged, the app turns your file into something it can reason about. This part is deterministic — fixed rules, no AI, the same result every time — and it runs in a fixed order:

  1. Ingest — the document arrives by manual upload, from the repository, or via Gmail intake, together with its details.
  2. Extract — Word and PDF files are read into plain text plus a record for every paragraph, keeping its numbering, heading style, table position, and any attached parts.
  3. Align — each paragraph gets a stable ID, index, and position so every later decision can point back to exactly where it came from.
  4. Map the structure — the app detects this document's own headings, numbering, ranges, and aliases, and builds a reference index. It never assumes "Clause 4" means a fixed thing — Clause 4 or Article II means whatever this NDA actually mapped it to.
  5. Resolve references — explicit cross-references (clause, article, section, schedule, annex, appendix, and lists like "Clauses 1, 1A and 2") are linked to the sections they point at, and each is marked resolved, partial, or unresolved.
  6. Classify concepts — each paragraph is tagged with the concept it carries: confidentiality definition, confidentiality obligation, use restriction, permitted disclosure, return/destruction, survival, and carve-outs. These tags roll up to the section that owns them.

Two safeguards matter here:

  • No made-up references. Headings that refer to themselves are skipped, and prose fragments like "this section introduces…" are not mistaken for a real "Section I" reference.
  • Substance over numbers. If a survival clause says "Articles 2, 3, 4 and 5 survive," the app can check whether those articles are actually confidentiality provisions — rather than trusting the sentence at face value.
Where the line is: this whole stage is pattern-based, not AI interpretation. Reading, mapping, references, and concept tags are deterministic inputs. The AI semantic judgement (Stage 3) runs afterwards as a separate layer. Ambiguous phrases like "the confidentiality obligations above" are deliberately left for the AI rather than guessed by rules.

Stage 2 — What each clause checker looks for

Each important clause has its own check with a clear job, its own inputs, and its own conditions for PASS, REVIEW, or FAIL. (FAIL is shown in the app as "Check" — the clause needs fixing.) Here's each one in plain terms:

Mutuality

Job: confirm the NDA binds both sides equally, not just a one-way "receiving party" model.

Passes when strong reciprocal-obligation language binds both parties as disclosing and receiving. Goes to Review on weak signals or role definitions without real reciprocal obligations. Fails on one-way / unilateral language. A safeguard: defining both roles is not enough on its own to pass — it's treated as something a human should confirm.

Confidential Information

Job: confirm the definition is broad enough and doesn't add exclusions that quietly weaken protection.

Passes when a broad definition covers enough required categories with no extra carve-outs. Goes to Review when coverage is broad but only implied, or when usage-right wording might weaken protection. Fails when the definition is missing, too narrow, or contains prohibited carve-outs. A safeguard: it checks whether an "independently developed" style qualification actually attaches to the carve-out before counting it.

Governing Law

Job: confirm there's a clear governing-law value and that it's one of your approved jurisdictions.

Passes when the law resolves to one on your approved list. Goes to Review on placeholder, conditional, heading-only, unresolved, or conflicting wording. Fails when a clear clause names a jurisdiction you don't approve. A safeguard: an approved law mentioned elsewhere (for example in a party's "incorporated under the laws of…" recital) does not rescue a non-approved governing law.

Term & Survival

Job: confirm ordinary confidentiality is time-limited (within your cap), while still allowing approved longer carve-outs (like trade secrets).

Passes when the ordinary term/survival is fixed and within the configured cap. Goes to Review when a cross-referenced survival scope is unresolved or not clearly ordinary confidentiality. Fails when the term is missing, over the cap, or indefinite outside allowed carve-outs. A safeguard: it inspects the actual resolved sections a survival clause points at, not assumed article numbers — and it won't be fooled into passing by unrelated wording like "claims survive for three years."

Non-Circumvention (a prohibited clause)

Job: confirm the NDA does not smuggle in non-circumvention, non-solicit, direct-dealing, substitute-purpose, or exclusivity restraints.

Passes when no operative restriction of that kind appears. Goes to Review on soft introduced-party / substitute-purpose / exclusivity language with no clear operative restraint. Fails when a definite restriction appears. A safeguard: lawful-circumvention references and negated wording (e.g. "may not include non-solicitation obligations") are correctly read as not a violation.

Signatures

Job: confirm there's an execution block complete enough for both sides to sign.

Passes when the block appears to include both parties, titles or capacities, and dates. Fails when it's missing or incomplete. This check is kept deliberately separate from the legal-concept verdicts — it's a structural check of the signing block, not a parse of who the signatories are.

How a clause decision is recorded (for auditors)

Every clause result is fully traceable. If you ever need to understand exactly why a clause landed where it did, the decision is laid out in a fixed reading order:

  1. Review state — pass, review, or check, plus whether it blocks sending.
  2. Reason code — a short machine-readable cause (e.g. approved_governing_law, weak_mutuality_signal, missing_required_clause) you can read before the prose.
  3. Evidence — the paragraph IDs, matched terms, and signal type behind the decision.
  4. Analysis — the clause-specific detail (for example the term/survival or governing-law analysis).
  5. Audit trace — the full path from input → evidence → classification → outputs → final decision.
Why so much detail? So that when an admin wants to change a rule, they can reconcile the reason code, the evidence, the analysis, and the trace first — and change the Playbook, never the engine. (See the Playbook tab.)

Stage 3 — The AI layer (semantic review)

For the meaning-heavy clauses — Mutuality, Confidential Information, Governing Law, Term & Survival, and Non-Circumvention — an AI reads the wording and judges it against your Playbook. (Signatures stay on the structural check above.) This is the layer that produces the verdicts you see.

  • What it's given: the clause requirements, your Playbook rules, the structure context from Stage 1, and the relevant source paragraphs.
  • Turning it on: AI review is enabled from the Admin tab, or configured at deployment by a system administrator.

Several guardrails sit around that AI judgement — they're the reason the verdicts are trustworthy:

Where it runs
Through a secure review service. The API keys live in backend configuration and are never exposed to anything running in your browser.
The model
A capable reasoning model is used by default, and which model runs is configurable at deployment by administrators — without touching the rest of the app.
Confidence gate
Each verdict carries a confidence score. If it's below the threshold (about 0.75 by default), the clause is pushed to Review instead of being trusted as a pass.
Citation check
Any pass or fail on a clause that's present must quote a sentence that genuinely appears in the document. Quotes that don't match the real text are rejected.
Fail-closed
If an assessment is missing, a citation is invalid, or the provider errors, the app does the safe thing — it escalates to Review and blocks auto-send rather than waving the document through.
Second opinion
An optional verifier pass can double-check the borderline cases the first AI was unsure about.

The net effect is the behaviour you saw on the previous tab: every verdict is grounded in a real quote, low confidence becomes "ask a human," and anything uncertain leans toward Review rather than a confident-sounding guess.

One source of truth. The AI never invents its own standards. It infers, judges, and applies your Playbook — and works to make documents comply to it. Change what's acceptable by editing the Playbook, not the engine.

Redlines

A redline is a tracked edit — a marked-up change showing exactly what should be added, removed, or replaced. It's the standard way lawyers exchange contract changes: deletions struck through, new text underlined.

Where redlines come from

  1. AI-proposed redlines — when a clause fails (or needs a fix), the AI suggests the corrected wording. For example, swapping an unapproved governing law for an approved one, or striking out a forbidden restriction.
  2. Your own edits — you can type directly into the document (in Redline view), and your changes are tracked as redlines too. You can also add comments to any paragraph using the small comment button that appears on it.

The four document views

A row of view buttons above the document changes how the markup is shown:

  • Redline — the original text with proposed edits shown as tracked changes (struck-through deletions, underlined insertions). This is the default.
  • Clean — the final text with all edits applied, markup hidden. This is what the document would look like if every change were accepted.
  • Side by Side — the original and the proposed version next to each other.
  • Original — the untouched source document, with your edits and comments hidden.

Including or ignoring individual redlines

You decide which proposed changes make it into the document you send. On each proposed edit you'll see two buttons:

  • Include — this change will be in the exported document.
  • Ignore — this change will be left out. A clause whose redline you've ignored shows a small Ignored in export tag so you can see at a glance what you've chosen to drop.
The AI proposes, but you dispose — nothing is auto-applied. You stay in control of every change that goes out.

The approve gate (the safety check before sending)

You cannot send an NDA out while clauses are still waiting on a human. This is the approve gate.

  • Each Review clause shows as a clickable pill. When you've checked it, click it to mark it Reviewed.
  • There's also a Reviewed button in the toolbar that marks all outstanding "needs review" clauses as reviewed at once (it asks you to confirm first, listing what it will change).
  • Once every flagged clause is acknowledged, the banner tells you "All review clauses marked reviewed. You can send the redline now."
  • The final Approve action lives on the matter's Overview tab footer.

Until that gate is satisfied, the Send Redline button stays disabled. (Note: even after you've reviewed everything, a document can still be unsendable for other reasons — a hard Fail, a missing recipient email, or the review being out of date — and the banner will tell you exactly which.)

Saving your work

  • Save Draft keeps your edits, comments, and Include/Ignore choices so you can come back later.
  • Reset Draft / Discard throws away unsaved changes (it warns you first and lists what would be lost).

Exporting the marked-up Word document

When you're happy with the changes:

  1. Click the Download button.
  2. A small menu appears showing DOCX (Word) and PDF options — plus a preview of exactly what the download will include (which clause redlines, your manual edits, and any comments).
  3. Choose DOCX to get the marked-up Word file with all your Included redlines and comments as real Word tracked changes and Word comments.

You can also click Send Redline to email the marked-up document straight back to the other side, with a summary of the changes auto-filled into the email body. (You confirm the recipient before it sends — see the Sending & E-Signature tab.)

One caveat to know: if the original NDA came in as a PDF (not a Word file), the Word document you export is rebuilt from the PDF and may not perfectly preserve the original formatting. The app tells you when this is the case so you're not surprised.

AI Search

The app has two search bars. They look similar but do different jobs — knowing which is which saves a lot of confusion.

A. The Dashboard search bar (the "smart" one)

This is the big search box on the Dashboard. Its placeholder reads Search or ask about your documents… and it has a little sparkle icon on the submit button, because it's AI-assisted: you can type a plain-English request, not just a keyword.

What it searches: every NDA in your workspace — the same list you see in the Repository (inbox NDAs, generated, uploaded, sent, and so on). It does not invent results; every result is a real document you own.

How it works:

  1. You type a request and press the arrow (or Enter).
  2. The app shows a brief Interpreting… message while it reads your request.
  3. The AI translates your words into a filter. A small Showing: … line appears explaining how it understood you.
  4. The matching documents appear as a list below.
  5. If the AI can't make sense of the request, it quietly falls back to a plain keyword match over your document titles and counterparty names — so the box always returns something useful, even for a bare name.

What you can do with each result:

  • Click the result to open that NDA in the workstation.
  • Summarize — produces a short AI summary of that NDA, shown inline and clearly labelled AI summary so it's never mistaken for verified fact. If it can't run, you get a friendly "Summary unavailable right now." message.
  • Relationships — shows that document's history/lineage as a simple timeline (original → AI redline / reviewed / generated → counterparty version), with the current version marked. This is factual, not AI — it just reads the versions the app already has. A brand-new document shows "No earlier versions yet."

Example searches:

  • documents still in review — lists NDAs that haven't finished review yet.
  • NDAs from Moorwand — typing just a company name filters to that counterparty's documents.
  • signed NDAs governed by English law — the AI reads this as a filter and shows matching signed agreements.

Entities & Signatories

A signing entity is one of your own company's legal companies — the specific corporate body that signs the NDA on your side. The entity is the master switch: pick it, and everything legal follows automatically.

What entities are

Large organisations aren't a single legal company — they're made up of several registered companies in different countries. For example, this app's company has entities such as:

  • Aspora Technology Services Private Limited (India)
  • Aspora Financial Services (IFSC) Private Limited (India, Gujarat)
  • Vance Money Services LLC (Delaware, USA)
  • Real Transfer Limited (United Kingdom)
  • Vance Techlabs Limited (Dubai, DIFC)
  • Vance Technologies Limited (United Kingdom)
  • Nesse Technologies Inc (Canada, Ontario)

Each signing entity is a self-contained bundle of facts: its legal name, its registered office address (some have more than one), its default governing law, its court, its jurisdiction of incorporation, and its signatory (who signs for it).

Why they exist

The right legal company has to sign the NDA, and the correct law and court must travel with it. If a UK entity is signing, the NDA must use UK law and UK courts — never, say, Delaware law by accident. By keeping each entity's facts bundled, the moment you pick the entity, the correct legal name, address, law, and court all come along automatically. You cannot accidentally pair the wrong law with the wrong company.

Why bundles matter. "Aspora Technology" (in Bengaluru) and "Aspora Financial Services" (in Gandhinagar) both use Indian law — but they sit in different cities, so their courts differ. Because the court is stored on each entity, picking one entity always gets that entity's own city's court, not the other's.

Signatories

A signatory is the actual person who signs the NDA on behalf of an entity. They have a name (e.g. "Daniyal Ahmad") and a title (their job role, e.g. "Director"). Each signing entity carries its own signatory, so when you pick an entity in the Generator, the signatory's name and title are filled in automatically into the signature block — you don't type them. If an entity doesn't yet have a named signatory, the document simply leaves blank signature lines for a person to sign by hand, rather than printing placeholder text.

Governing law & court (jurisdiction)

  • Governing law — which country or state's law the NDA follows (e.g. "India," "Delaware," "England and Wales"). If there's ever a disagreement, this is the body of law used to interpret the contract.
  • Court / jurisdiction — which courts would actually hear a dispute (e.g. "courts in Bengaluru, Karnataka" or "the DIFC Courts"). The law and the court go together but are not the same thing.

Every signing entity has a default law and court, written automatically into the NDA's "Governing law and jurisdiction" clause. In the Generator they appear as read-only text — you see them, but you don't pick them.

Approved laws only

Your company keeps a list of approved laws in its playbook. Today the approved set is:

  • India
  • Delaware
  • England and Wales
  • DIFC (the Dubai International Financial Centre, a common-law zone with its own courts)
  • Ontario, Canada

Only these laws are allowed, and the app enforces it:

  • An administrator cannot save an entity whose law isn't on the approved list — the save is rejected with a clear error.
  • The Generator will not produce an NDA using an unapproved law. Generation is refused rather than quietly producing a non-compliant document.
  • A guard also requires the court to match the law's jurisdiction — you can't end up with, say, an English court paired with DIFC law. A mismatch is blocked before anything is signed.

How it all ties together

The signing entity is the master switch. Pick the entity, and everything legal follows automatically:

  1. Legal name and address — filled from the entity's bundle.
  2. Signatory — the entity's named signer (and title) is placed in the signature block.
  3. Governing law — set to the entity's default approved law.
  4. Court — set to the entity's own court.

You still control the deal details: the other party's name and address, the purpose, and the term (within the playbook's cap). But the legal details — who signs, under which law, in which court — are coupled to the entity so they can never be mismatched.

Where entities are managed

Day-to-day, you just pick an entity in the Generator. Entities themselves are set up and edited by an administrator in the Admin → Entities console. A regular user sees the list read-only; only an admin can add or change entities. Their governing law and court are managed in the Playbook editor's Entities & Courts table, so there's one single source of truth.

The Inbox (auto-receiving NDAs)

The app can watch a connected Gmail inbox, notice emails that contain an NDA, and pull those NDAs into the app for you — without you uploading anything by hand.

There are two ways an NDA gets into the app: you upload it yourself (drag a Word or PDF file in), or the app fetches it from email — this is the Inbox, and it's what this section is about.

Step by step: how the Inbox works

  1. You connect a Gmail account. An administrator connects the company's NDA inbox (for example, a shared address like legal@yourcompany.com) to the app once. After that, the app has permission to read that inbox.
  2. The app checks the inbox on a schedule. Every so often (by default, every 10 minutes — adjustable), the app looks at recent emails.
  3. It looks for emails with document attachments. It only considers emails that are in the inbox (not sent by you), have a Word (.docx) or PDF attachment, and arrived within the look-back window (default 90 days — an editable admin setting; see Admin).
  4. It decides which of those are actually NDAs. An invoice or sales deck that happens to have an attachment is not pulled in. A real NDA is. (See "How it decides" below.)
  5. It pulls the NDA in and creates a "matter" for it. A matter is just the app's word for one NDA it's tracking. The new matter shows up in your Inbox list.
  6. It reviews the NDA in the background. It imports the NDA quickly, then runs the slower, careful AI review a little later, in the background (see below).
  7. You get a notification. When new NDAs arrive, you see a small pop-up — for example "New NDA from Acme Corp," or "3 new NDAs in your inbox."

How it decides an email is an NDA ("detection")

The app doesn't just trust the subject line. It looks at several things and weighs them up:

  • The words in the email — subject, body, and the preview snippet. Words like "non-disclosure agreement," "NDA," "mutual NDA," or "confidentiality agreement" are strong signals.
  • The attachment's file name — e.g. Mutual-NDA-Acme.docx is a strong signal.
  • The text inside the attachment — the app actually opens the document and reads it. This matters because many NDAs are forwarded with a bland email body ("Please see attached for review") where the only proof is inside the file itself.

It also actively rules things out, in layers, cheapest first — so the paid AI classifier only ever sees emails that already look like real NDAs:

  • Noise senders are excluded up front. Automated mail from e-signature platforms (DocuSign, Adobe Sign / EchoSign, HelloSign, PandaDoc) and Google Calendar notifications is filtered out before anything else, so "your document is ready to sign," signing reminders, and calendar invites never create phantom NDAs. This exclusion list is an admin setting with those senders as its default; the DocuSign family is always excluded as a hard floor.
  • Obvious non-NDAs are gated out before the paid AI runs. A fast, built-in check reads each attachment and drops clear non-NDAs — proposals, invoices, purchase orders, pricing decks, questionnaires — before the app spends a paid AI call classifying them. A genuine NDA that merely mentions a "proposal" in passing is not wrongly dropped: an explicit NDA signal (a name like Mutual-NDA.docx, or clear NDA wording) is an escape hatch that always sends the document on to the AI.
  • Executed NDAs that arrive via an e-sign platform are still captured. A signing platform's completion email is sometimes the only copy of a fully-signed NDA that ever reaches the mailbox. So when one of those otherwise-excluded platform emails carries an explicit NDA signal, it's let through — but always routed to the human-triage lane and flagged, never silently auto-imported. A person looks at it, because it's usually an already-executed document.

If a document is borderline — some NDA wording but not enough to be confident — the app doesn't silently throw it away. It routes it to a human-review lane so a person can take a look rather than losing it.

Example. An email arrives with the subject "Please review and sign" and an attachment named Agreement-Final.pdf. The body says nothing about NDAs. The app opens the PDF, sees "Confidentiality Agreement," "Disclosing Party," and "Receiving Party" inside it, and correctly decides it is an NDA — even though the email itself gave no hint.

Why review runs in the background (not instantly)

When an NDA is imported, the app records it right away but defers the heavy AI review to run a little later. Reading an NDA and judging every clause with AI is slow and uses real computing power. If 20 NDAs all arrived at once and the app tried to fully review them all at the same instant, it could overload itself. So instead, the app:

  • imports each NDA quickly (so you see it appear),
  • adds it to a queue (a waiting line) of NDAs to be reviewed,
  • and works through that queue steadily, usually one at a time.

While an NDA is waiting its turn, it shows as Not Reviewed Yet. Once the background review finishes, its verdict appears. This is normal and intentional — the app being careful, not the app being broken.

The Playbook (your rulebook)

The Playbook is the single, central description of what your company considers acceptable in an NDA. It's the most important concept in the whole app.

The Playbook is the one source of truth for the rules. The AI reads the Playbook and judges every NDA against it. If you change a rule in the Playbook, the AI's judgments change to match — automatically. You don't need to change any code, retrain anything, or email anyone.

Why this matters

  • Every NDA is judged consistently — by the same written rules, every time.
  • You are in control. The AI doesn't have its own private opinions about what a "good" NDA is. It applies your rules.
  • Changes are instant and global. Decide to accept a new governing law? Add it to the Playbook once, and every future NDA is judged with that law allowed.

What's in the Playbook

The Playbook is organised as a list of clauses — the specific parts of an NDA the app cares about. Out of the box, these are:

  1. Mutuality — does the NDA bind both sides equally, or only one side?
  2. Confidential Information — is "confidential information" defined broadly enough, without sneaky carve-outs that weaken protection?
  3. Governing Law — which country's / state's law governs? (Must be one of your approved jurisdictions.)
  4. Term and Survival — how long do the confidentiality obligations last? (Your default cap is five years for ordinary confidentiality.)
  5. Non-Circumvention — the NDA must not sneak in extra business restrictions like non-competes, non-solicits, exclusivity, IP-grabs, or penalty clauses. (This one is a prohibited clause.)
  6. Signatures — does the signing block name both parties, their titles, and a date?

Each clause carries the same kinds of information, in plain English:

  • What good looks like — the standard/position you want.
  • What to watch for — the wording that should raise a flag.
  • Suggested fix language — the wording the app can drop in to correct the document (a redline).

"Required" vs "Prohibited" clauses

Every clause has a stance:

  • Required — this should be present and correct. The app flags it if it's missing or deficient (e.g. a Governing Law clause pointing at the wrong country).
  • Prohibited — this should not appear at all. The app flags it if it's present (e.g. a non-compete buried inside an NDA).

"Approved options" (a list of what's allowed)

Some clauses hold a list of allowed choices — called approved options. The clearest example is Governing Law. By default that list is:

  • India
  • Delaware
  • England & Wales (marked as the preferred default)
  • DIFC (Dubai International Financial Centre)
  • Ontario, Canada
Example. An NDA governed by "the laws of Texas" gets flagged — Texas isn't on your approved list. An NDA governed by "England and Wales" passes. If you later add Texas to the approved-options list, the very next Texas NDA passes instead. You control the list; the AI just applies it.

Pass / Review / Fail — the three outcomes

For each clause, the Playbook defines the conditions that lead to one of three verdicts:

  • PASS — the clause meets your rules. Nothing to do.
  • REVIEW — the wording is unclear, conditional, or borderline. A human should look.
  • FAIL — the clause clearly breaks a rule (e.g. an unapproved governing law, or a prohibited non-compete). It needs fixing.

So a single NDA produces a clause-by-clause report card: each clause gets a Pass, Review, or Fail, all decided by the rules you wrote in the Playbook.

How the AI uses the Playbook

When the app reviews an NDA, it hands the AI the text of the NDA and your Playbook rules for each clause. The AI's job is to judge the NDA against your Playbook — clause by clause — and report Pass, Review, or Fail with a quote from the document as evidence. It's making the document comply to your rules; it never invents rules of its own and never quietly overrides the Playbook.

The punchline: if you want the app to judge NDAs differently, you change the Playbook, not the AI. The Playbook is the steering wheel.

  • Personalisation — your signature — your sign-off, signature name, and full block. Every user sets their own; an admin can set the shared default.
  • Drive — connection and auto-filing — connect Google Drive, choose a folder, toggle auto-filing of new matters.
  • Admin Access — who can administer — add or remove in-app admins by email (see the bootstrap caveat below).

Important caveats / known limits

"Inbound search terms" are informational by default. The field saves your words and shows them back, but by default detection uses a fixed, built-in vocabulary — your saved terms don't change which inbound emails get detected and imported as NDAs. In plain terms: the inbox scan fetches a structural envelope (every Word/PDF attachment in the window that you didn't send) — the keyword list isn't part of that fetch. After fetching, the system scores each attachment for "NDA-ness" using that fixed, built-in vocabulary baked into the code, not the words you typed. So your saved terms are stored and displayed as a hint, but no detection decision reads them. Editing the field is safe — it just won't change behaviour. (An opt-in mode that lets your custom terms extend detection now exists but is off unless a deployment enables it; until it's turned on, the built-in vocabulary is what decides. The fetch query and how many messages are fetched never change either way — custom terms can only add content-scorer signals, never widen the mailbox scan.)
Some settings can be "pinned" by the deployment. A few controls can be overridden by the environment the app runs in; when that happens the UI says so and locks the control. For example: the AI Runtime engine can be pinned ("Runtime settings are pinned by the backend environment" — your save is ignored by design); the AI key source can be an environment key that clearing the in-app key won't remove; the Gmail import limit falls back to an environment default when no in-app value is set.
Changes apply to NEW work, not past work. Turning AI on/off, changing the engine, or editing the Playbook affects future reviews. It does not retroactively re-judge NDAs already reviewed.
"Bootstrap" admins cannot be removed in-app. Admins set in the deployment (labelled Bootstrap) are immutable — there's no Remove button. Only admins added in-app can be removed. The app also prevents you from removing the last admin / locking yourself out.
The Gmail master toggle is the real stop. It's the actual on/off for scheduled sync. The per-role inbound/outbound rows are secondary; when the master sync is off, those rows are inert no matter how they're configured.

Operator tools (admin API, not buttons in the app)

Two admin capabilities exist as secured API endpoints for an operator rather than as buttons on a screen — they're powerful, occasional, and deliberately not one click away:

  • Bulk-archive of Gmail-sourced matters — a cleanup for the phantom matters that automated email can accumulate. It runs as a dry-run preview first (it returns exactly which matters would be archived, without touching anything), and only archives once you confirm that exact selection back. Every archived matter (and its source document) is copied to an archive area before deletion, so nothing is lost — and the archived emails are recorded so a later inbox scan won't just re-import them. As a safety interlock, the execute step refuses to run while inbound Gmail import is still on (you turn inbound off first), so a poll can't race the cleanup.
  • Backup export — downloads the matter records (full extracted NDA text and metadata) as a JSON file, either for a single owner or, with the all-owners scope, across every owner. Because it contains full document text, it's an admin-only, sensitive export.

Both are audited admin actions run by an operator; a regular user never sees them in the interface.

What you cannot change from the app (the "backend")

These are set by engineers in the deployment configuration ("environment variables") or hardcoded — deliberately, for safety and consistency:

  • Which AI models are used — the reviewer, verifier, intake classifier, and structure model are chosen by environment configuration. You choose whether AI runs (the toggle); you don't pick the model by clicking.
  • The NDA-detection vocabulary and "floor" — the built-in keyword tables and the minimum score an attachment must reach to be treated as an NDA (and the lower bar for routing to human triage) are hardcoded, so detection stays consistent across every account.
  • The Gmail fetch query structure — the shape of the inbox search ("attachments only, not from me, within the window") is fixed in the code. (The window length in days is editable — see above.)
  • The import-limit ceiling (max 40) — you set the per-poll limit, but the upper cap of 40 is fixed to protect Gmail's rate limits.
  • The AI verifier (second-opinion pass) — driven by the environment, not a UI switch. It's active only when configured in the deployment and a key is present.
  • The AI confidence threshold and target clauses — come from environment configuration / the Playbook; shown read-only in AI Runtime.
  • The deterministic (rules-based) checks — the non-AI structural checks are part of the program logic, not settings, so results stay repeatable regardless of AI state.

Why are these locked? Two reasons: safety (a wrong value could miss real NDAs, leak documents, or hit rate limits) and consistency (every account behaves the same, so results are predictable and auditable). Engineers change these through configuration with review — not through a click anyone could make by accident.

Safety notes

  • The Gmail master toggle is your emergency stop. To stop the system pulling in and processing inbound NDAs, turn off Gmail sync. Disconnecting a single account is not the same as stopping sync.
  • The Playbook is the safe place to change review behaviour. If you want a clause treated differently, edit the Playbook — the intended, reviewed path, not the locked detection/AI internals.
  • Changing detection or AI behaviour is sensitive. Detection vocabulary, thresholds, the fetch window structure, and model choice are intentionally not in the UI. If you think one is wrong, that's an engineering change — raise it rather than looking for a hidden switch.
  • Adding an AI key turns on real (paid) AI calls. Clearing the in-app key (or turning AI off) stops in-app AI calls — but remember an environment key can keep AI configured regardless.
  • Admin Access is privileged. Anyone you add as an admin can change the rulebook, entities, and connections. Add sparingly; bootstrap admins are immutable by design.

Glossary

Every term used in this guide, in plain English, alphabetised.

A
Admin
A signed-in user allowed into the settings console to change the rulebook, signing entities, connections, and who else can administer. Most admin sections are hidden from non-admins, who see a "managed by an administrator" message instead.
AI model
The specific artificial-intelligence system that does a job (reviewing a clause, classifying an inbound email, etc.). You can choose whether AI runs, but which model is set by the environment, not by clicking.
AI summary
A short, on-demand AI-written summary of a single NDA, shown inline from a search result via the Summarize button. Always labelled "AI summary" so it's never mistaken for verified fact.
Approve gate
The safety check that stops you sending an NDA while clauses still need a human. You mark each flagged clause (or all of them) as Reviewed; the final Approve action lives on the matter's Overview tab. Until the gate is satisfied, Send Redline stays disabled.
Approved law
A governing law on the company's allowed list (currently India, Delaware, England and Wales, DIFC, and Ontario, Canada). Only approved laws may be used; the app refuses to generate or save an NDA using a law outside this list.
Approved option
A list of allowed choices inside a clause. The clearest example is the list of approved governing laws. An NDA that picks something off the list (e.g. Texas law) gets flagged.
B
Background processing / Background review
Work the app does on its own, server-side, without you waiting — chiefly importing inbound NDAs and running the AI review. A new NDA is imported quickly, then queued and reviewed a little later (usually one at a time) to avoid overload. Until its turn comes it shows "Not Reviewed Yet"; a toast notifies you when work finishes.
C
Clause
One numbered chunk of the contract covering a single topic (e.g. Governing Law, Term, Confidential Information). The AI reviews the NDA one clause at a time, and the Playbook carries a rule for each clause it cares about (Mutuality, Confidential Information, Governing Law, Term and Survival, Non-Circumvention, Signatures).
Confidence
A behind-the-scenes score for how sure the AI is about a clause's verdict. Low confidence pushes a clause toward Review rather than letting a shaky pass or fail stand.
Corpus
Your read-only, searchable library (the "filing cabinet") of finished, signed (executed) NDAs. By default it shows only executed agreements, with a toggle to include in-progress ones. You browse and filter it; you don't edit documents here.
Counterparty
The other company on the NDA — the party you're signing the agreement with. In the Generator you enter its name, and optionally its email, address, and a description of its business.
Court / jurisdiction
Which courts would actually hear a dispute about the NDA (e.g. "courts in Bengaluru, Karnataka" or "the DIFC Courts"). It travels with the signing entity alongside the governing law, and the two must belong to the same legal system.
D
Detection
How the app decides whether an email actually contains an NDA. It weighs the email's words (subject, body, preview), the attachment's file name, and the text inside the attached document — and filters out non-NDAs like invoices, sales decks, and e-signature notifications.
Detection floor
The minimum "NDA-ness" score an inbox attachment must reach before the system treats it as an NDA worth importing (with a lower bar for routing to human triage). This floor is hardcoded for consistency and safety, and is not an editable setting.
E
E-signature / DocuSign
Sending a finalised NDA out to be signed electronically via DocuSign. Both parties (the counterparty and the Aspora signatory) sign in DocuSign by email; the app tracks status and offers a "Download signed" link when complete.
Envelope (DocuSign)
The DocuSign package containing the document sent for signature. The app shows its ID and status, prevents accidental duplicate envelopes, and polls until signing is finished.
Environment variable
A configuration value set in the deployment (the server the app runs on), not in the app's screens. Engineers use these for things like which AI model runs, the AI key, or the active review engine. When a setting is controlled this way, the UI usually says it is "pinned by the backend environment" and locks the control.
Executed
Fully signed by both sides. An executed NDA leaves the active board and moves into the Corpus library.
F
Fail
Verdict meaning the clause clearly breaks a Playbook rule (a required clause is missing, a clause is present but wrong, or a forbidden clause is present). The app flags it and proposes a redline to fix it. Shown in red.
Flag (feature flag)
A switch, usually set in the environment, that turns a piece of behaviour on or off without changing the app's screens. Some behaviour (for example, the optional setting that lets your custom "inbound search terms" influence detection, or the AI verifier pass) depends on such a switch being enabled in the deployment — so a field can appear in the UI yet have no effect until the switch is on. The inbound-search-terms switch is off by default; today those terms are informational and detection uses a fixed, built-in vocabulary.
G
Generate
To create a brand-new, finished NDA document automatically from a template, by pressing "Generate NDA" in the Generator tab. You fill in a short form; the app produces a downloadable Word (.docx) file.
Global toggle (Gmail polling switch)
The single master on/off switch for scheduled Gmail sync. Turning it off is the safety stop: the app stops checking the inbox entirely and pulls in nothing new. NDAs already in the app are unaffected. Disconnecting one mailbox is not the same as flipping this toggle.
Governing law
Which country's or state's law the NDA is interpreted under (e.g. India, Delaware, England and Wales), set automatically by the signing entity. Shown read-only in the Generator.
Grounding
The rule that the AI must quote the exact sentence from the document that supports its verdict. The app checks those quotes against the real text, so the AI can't make things up. An unsupported verdict on a present clause is bumped to human review.
I
Inbox / Intake
The app's ability to receive NDAs automatically by reading a connected Gmail account, instead of you uploading each file by hand. "Intake" is the moment an NDA enters the app and becomes something it is tracking.
Include / Ignore
The two controls on each proposed redline. Include puts the change into the exported document; Ignore leaves it out (marked "Ignored in export"). The AI proposes, but you decide what actually goes out.
Intake Queue
The Dashboard table listing inbound NDAs that have arrived and need attention.
M
Matter
The app's name for one NDA and everything attached to it — the document, its AI review, redlines, status, and history. Each imported or uploaded NDA becomes a matter.
N
NDA
A non-disclosure agreement: a contract where two parties promise to keep each other's confidential information secret.
Notification / Toast
A small card that slides in at the top-right of the screen to tell you something happened in the background (a new NDA arrived, a review finished or failed, a document was generated). Auto-dismisses after about 12 seconds; click to act on it, or × to close.
P
Pass
Verdict meaning the clause meets your Playbook's rule — nothing to do. Shown in green.
Playbook
Your company's master rulebook — the single source of truth for all the rules, approved options, the maximum term, and the standard clause positions. The AI reads it and judges every NDA against it; change a rule and the AI's judgments change to match.
R
Recipient confirmation
A safety step that requires you to confirm the exact email address before an NDA goes out. The server refuses to send if the confirmed address doesn't match the document's intended recipient — a guard against sending to the wrong or a spoofed address.
Redline
A tracked edit — a marked-up change to the contract showing exactly what to add, remove, or replace. Deletions are struck through and new text is underlined, so the other side can see precisely what changed. The standard way contract changes are exchanged.
Repository
The six-column board where your active NDAs live and move from "arrived" to "sent" as you work them (Generated, Upload, Inbox, In Review, Reviewed, Sent).
Required vs Prohibited (clause stance)
A "required" clause should be present and correct (flagged if missing/wrong); a "prohibited" clause should not appear at all (flagged if present, e.g. a non-compete).
Review
The AI's clause-by-clause read of an NDA, comparing each part against your Playbook and giving a verdict. Also the amber verdict (see next entry).
Review (verdict)
Verdict meaning the AI isn't confident enough to decide, so a human should look before sending (the wording is ambiguous, conditional, or conflicting). Not a failure. Shown in amber. A Review clause blocks sending until a person acknowledges it.
Review workstation
The screen where one NDA is open — the document with highlighted clauses on the left, and the clause list with verdicts, evidence, reasoning, comments, and proposed redlines on the right.
Rule / Condition
The written test inside a clause that produces a verdict. Each clause defines conditions that lead to Pass (meets the rules), Review (unclear — a human should check), or Fail (clearly breaks a rule).
S
Search facet
A clickable filter group in the Corpus's left-hand rail (e.g. Governing law, Mutuality, Term length, Stage, Flags). Select values to narrow the list; each selection becomes a removable chip (token) in the search bar. Facets stack together to combine filters.
Send Document
The Dashboard flow for emailing a .docx document to a counterparty as-is (no review or redlining), through your connected Gmail. The sent item appears in the Repository's Sent column.
Setting (vs hardcoded)
A "setting" is something you change by clicking in the app; it's saved and takes effect for new work. "Hardcoded" means the value is fixed in the program and can only be changed by engineers. The Playbook rules, Gmail sync frequency, and the inbound window (days) are settings; the NDA-detection vocabulary and the import-limit ceiling of 40 are hardcoded.
Signatory
The actual person who signs the NDA on behalf of a signing entity, identified by a name and a title (job role). Filled in automatically based on the entity you choose.
Signing entity
One of your own company's legal companies that signs the NDA on your side (e.g. "Aspora Technology Services Private Limited"). Each entity bundles its legal name, address, default governing law, court, and signatory, so picking it fills all of them in at once — and fixes the governing law and court.
Signing order
Whether signers sign one after another (sequential — set with the up/down arrows on each signer row) or at the same time (parallel).
Single source of truth
The idea that the Playbook — and only the Playbook — defines the rules. The AI has no opinions of its own; it applies the Playbook. To change how NDAs are judged, you edit the Playbook, not the AI.
Stalled / still reviewing
A review that hasn't finished yet shows as "Reviewing…" and is not a failure — the app keeps checking and shows the result when ready. If a review runs past the usual window (~4 minutes) the copy switches to a calm "still working, hang tight" reassurance; if its recorded start time gets old (the server's ~10-minute window) it reports a distinct "stalled / interrupted, retry" state. Only a genuine, recorded error shows as "failed," and there is always a Retry button.
Status
Where an NDA currently sits in its lifecycle; on the board this is the column it's in (Generated, Upload, Inbox, In Review, Reviewed, or Sent).
Sync frequency
How often the app checks the connected inbox for new NDAs. Options range from "Always on" (about every minute) to every 2 hours; the default is every 10 minutes. Separate from the sync window and the import limit.
Sync window (inbound window)
How far back in time the app looks when scanning the inbox. It is an editable admin setting (in days), defaulting to 90 days and settable from 1 to about 3,650 days. Older email is ignored.
T
Template
The company's standard, pre-written NDA document (the "Generic NDA"). It supplies the fixed structure and boilerplate legal wording; the Generator fills in the blanks (party names, term, law, etc.) for each new deal.
Term
How long the NDA stays in force, in years. Set with a +/− stepper in the Generator. The minimum is 1 year and the maximum is capped by the playbook (currently 5 years); a larger number is automatically reduced to the cap.
V
Verdict
The AI's outcome for a single clause. Every important clause gets one of three: Pass, Review, or Fail.
Verifier
An optional second AI pass that double-checks the borderline cases the first AI was unsure about — a built-in second opinion. It runs only when configured in the deployment (there is no in-app toggle).
